Abstract

Iron loss, also known as core loss, is a phenomenon in which energy is dissipated in the form of heat in the iron core of electrical machines, such as transformers and electric motors. This loss can be significant, particularly in high-frequency applications, and can lead to decreased efficiency and increased operating costs. In this review, we will explore the historical overview, mechanism, and constitutive equations of iron loss, as well as recent advancements, key challenges, and relevant technologies in the field.

Mechanism

Iron loss is caused by eddy currents, which are circulating currents that are induced in the iron core of an electrical machine by an alternating magnetic field. These eddy currents generate heat, which leads to energy dissipation and iron loss. The magnitude of the eddy currents and, therefore, the iron loss is influenced by several factors, including the frequency of the magnetic field, the permeability of the iron core, and the conductivity of the iron. Constitutive equations, such as the Jiles-Atherton model, have been developed to describe the relationship between these factors and the iron loss.
