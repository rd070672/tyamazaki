Elastocaloric cooling

Abstract

Elastocaloric cooling is a method of cooling that utilizes the change in temperature of a material as it is deformed. This change in temperature, known as the elastocaloric effect, is based on the adiabatic temperature change of a material due to a change in its mechanical strain. Elastocaloric cooling has the potential to be a more efficient and environmentally friendly alternative to traditional cooling methods such as vapor compression refrigeration.

Histrical Overview

The elastocaloric effect was first discovered in 1834 by French physicist Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot, who observed that the temperature of a metal rod changed as it was stretched or compressed. However, it was not until the early 21st century that researchers began to explore the potential of elastocaloric cooling as a practical technology. In 2006, a team of researchers at the Max Planck Institute for Intelligent Systems in Stuttgart, Germany, demonstrated the first elastocaloric cooling device. Since then, research in this field has rapidly progressed, with numerous studies investigating the potential of different materials and configurations for elastocaloric cooling.

Mechanism

The elastocaloric effect is based on the adiabatic temperature change of a material as it is deformed. When a material is stretched or compressed, its internal energy changes, leading to a change in temperature. The magnitude of this change in temperature is determined by the material's specific heat capacity and the change in strain. The constitutive equations that describe the elastocaloric effect are based on the thermodynamic principles of adiabatic processes and the mechanical properties of the material.
